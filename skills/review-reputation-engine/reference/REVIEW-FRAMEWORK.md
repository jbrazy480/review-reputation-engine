# The Review and Reputation Framework

The method behind this skill, the CLI, and the example library. By **James Hill ("The AI Guy")**, founder of **[RizzDial](https://rizzdial.com)**. More at **[aiguyofficial.com](https://aiguyofficial.com)**.

Reviews are local lead flow. A business with more recent five-star reviews shows higher in the map pack, earns more clicks, and converts more of them. Most businesses do not have a review problem because their service is bad. They have a review problem because nobody asks, nobody asks at the right moment, and nobody responds to the reviews that come in.

This framework fixes all three: ask the right person, at the right moment, the right way, and respond to every review fast.

## The three jobs of a review system

1. **Generate:** ask every happy customer for a review, right after the win.
2. **Protect:** catch unhappy customers privately before they post in public.
3. **Respond:** reply to every review, good and bad, fast and human.

A good system does all three automatically, so reviews are not left to luck.

## The honest line on review gating

There is a right way and a wrong way to handle unhappy customers.

- **Allowed and smart:** ask everyone "how did we do" first, then make it easy for happy customers to leave a public review, and route unhappy customers to a private message so you can fix the problem. You are asking everyone and you are not faking anything.
- **Not allowed:** hiding or blocking negative reviews, paying for reviews, writing fake reviews, or only inviting hand-picked customers. Google prohibits review gating that selectively suppresses negative public reviews, and platforms remove fake reviews. Do not do it.

This framework uses the smart, allowed version: ask everyone, route by sentiment, fix problems privately, never fake or suppress.

## The 5 part framework

### 1. The Ask (timing and trigger)

The single biggest lever is when you ask. Ask right after the win, while the good feeling is fresh:

- Service business: when the job is marked complete.
- Appointment business: a few hours after the visit.
- Sale: shortly after delivery or onboarding.

One trigger fires the sequence. Never ask cold weeks later.

### 2. The Channels

- **SMS:** highest open and reply rate, best for the first ask.
- **Email:** good for a second touch and for customers who prefer it.
- Keep it to two touches plus one reminder. More than that annoys.

### 3. The Smart Routing (the feedback gate, done right)

The first message asks a simple question, not for a review yet:

- "How did we do today, 1 to 5?"
- 4 or 5: send the direct Google review link and a one-line thank you. Make it one tap.
- 1 to 3: route to a private reply ("what went wrong, we want to make it right"), alert the owner, and fix it. Do not send these to the public review page.

You asked everyone. You suppressed nothing. You just gave unhappy customers a private path first.

### 4. The Copy (what each message says)

Rules that make review-ask copy convert:

- Reference the specific job or visit, so it feels personal.
- Make the public review a single tap with a direct link.
- Keep it short and warm, like a text from a person.
- Thank them first, ask second.
- For the reminder, a light nudge, never pushy, and easy to ignore.

### 5. The Response Library (reply to every review)

Reply to all reviews, fast. The replies are public, so they speak to future customers too:

- **Five star:** thank by name, mention the specific service, invite them back. Short.
- **Neutral (three to four):** thank them, acknowledge the gap, offer to make it better, move it to private.
- **Negative (one to two):** stay calm and human, apologize for the experience, do not argue or get defensive, take it offline with a real contact, and show future readers you handle problems well. Never reveal private details.

Speed matters. A fast, gracious reply to a bad review often does more for future customers than the bad review hurts.

## The Build Spec (how it runs)

Map each step to an automation in a CRM. This works with GoHighLevel and other platforms:

- A trigger (job complete, appointment done, sale closed) starts the workflow.
- Step 1 sends the "how did we do" SMS.
- A branch routes 4 to 5 to the Google review link, and 1 to 3 to a private reply plus an owner alert.
- A reminder fires if there is no response.
- Review monitoring feeds new reviews to the response library, with the owner approving replies.
- Tags track sentiment and whether a review was left.

## Ship checklist

- The ask fires right after the win, not weeks later.
- Smart routing asks everyone and suppresses nothing.
- Happy customers get a one-tap Google link.
- Unhappy customers get a private path and an owner alert.
- Two touches plus one reminder, no more.
- A reply for every review, fast and human.
- Never fake, never pay for, never hide reviews.
- Every step mapped to a CRM trigger.

More reviews means more inbound, which feeds your booking and follow-up systems.
